# If You Are Personally Opposed But Don't Want It Illegal

## You are likely here if you believe

- Abortion is wrong — you wouldn't have one yourself, and you'd counsel a loved one against it.
- But you don't think it's your place to force that conviction on someone else.
- Laws based on private morality or religious belief don't belong in a pluralist society.
- Education, adoption, contraception, and support for single mothers are the right responses — not criminalization.
- Banning abortion causes more harm than it prevents: women imprisoned, back-alley procedures, lives ruined.

## The question your position cannot answer

Either abortion is the killing of a human being or it isn't. If it isn't, your "personal opposition" has no object — you are against nothing. If it is, then "I wouldn't do it myself but I think it should be legal for others" is a position you hold about nothing else. You would not say you are personally opposed to murdering toddlers but support the right of parents to make that choice. The preborn child is either a human being who deserves protection, or the personal opposition is an aesthetic preference with no moral content.

The "imposing morality" framing is a category error. Every law imposes some moral claim. The speed limit imposes the morality that you may not risk others' lives for your convenience. The law against theft imposes the morality that property may not be taken by force. The law against murder imposes the morality that one person may not kill another. The question was never whether morality will be legislated. The question, as the FAQ states plainly, is whose morality will be legislated — and in the case of abortion, whether the law will protect the weakest party or license their killing.

"Personally opposed, politically permissive" is not a neutral middle ground. It is the position that the killing of a human being should remain available at the discretion of the one doing the killing. Name it that clearly before you read further.

What you're being called to is something different from the movement you may have assumed was handling this.

## The specific objection the corpus answers

> "I don't want to impose my beliefs on others."

Every law imposes a moral framework on others. The law against murder imposes the belief that one person may not kill another — including on those who disagree. You are not choosing between imposing morality and being neutral. You are choosing which morality wins. The FAQ answers this directly: "The question is not whether morality will be legislated but whose morality will be legislated." **Read [the FAQ](/docs/abolitionistsrising.com/faq), §2.**
