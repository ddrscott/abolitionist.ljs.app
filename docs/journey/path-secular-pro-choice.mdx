# If You Support Legal Abortion

## You are likely here if you believe

- A woman's body is her own, and no government or religion has the right to override that.
- The preborn is not yet a person — personhood requires consciousness, viability, or some threshold you haven't quite defined.
- Religious arguments against abortion don't belong in public law in a pluralist society.
- Banning abortion just drives it underground and kills women.
- You haven't studied the question deeply — it's just obvious that most people should have this choice.

## The question your position cannot answer

When a sperm and egg fuse, the result is a genetically distinct human organism with its own DNA — sometimes a different blood type than the mother, sometimes a different sex. This is not a religious claim. It is what embryologists have written in standard textbooks for decades. The scientific question is not contested by anyone who actually works in human embryology. The FAQ article below quotes Moore, Langman, Larsen, O'Rahilly, Carlson, Kischer, and others — researchers arguing from biology, not theology.

Once the scientific question is conceded, the argument usually retreats to personhood: *fine, it's biologically human, but it isn't a person yet.* The problem is that every threshold used to define personhood — consciousness, viability, sentience — applied consistently, strips legal protection from infants, from the sleeping, and from the cognitively disabled. That is not a reductio invented by pro-lifers; it is where the logic lands, as these texts make plain.

The bodily-autonomy argument fares no better. Bodily autonomy has never been absolute. Laws against toxic dumping, parental neglect, and assault all constrain what you may do with your body when another person is harmed. The question is not whether your body may be constrained — law does that routinely — but whether the being inside you is the kind of being whose death the law should prevent.

Finally, "you can't legislate morality" is not a position — it is a mistake. Every law legislates someone's moral claim. The question is always *whose* morality, not *whether*.

## The specific objection the corpus answers

> "My body, my choice."

The body inside a pregnant mother is not her body. It has distinct DNA, often a different blood type, and sometimes a different sex. Bodily autonomy is not a license to kill a dependent — no jurisdiction treats it as such — and the four-point rebuttal to the forced-organ-donation analogy specifically dismantles the strongest version of this argument. **See the FAQ entries on [bodily autonomy](/docs/abolitionistsrising.com/faq).**
