# Reader Journey — Question Tree (Design Doc)

**Status:** Locked blueprint for Tasks 3–10 of the reader-journey-guide plan.
**Purpose:** Map every entry-point reader to a faithful, corpus-grounded path that ends at either the faithful-abolitionist terminus or (for the non-Christian path) at the gospel.
**Do not re-derive.** If a path-writer's instincts disagree with this doc, they must re-read the cited source articles before deviating. The corpus is ground truth.

---

## How to use this doc (for Task 4–10 writers)

Each entry node below supplies the six ingredients needed to fill the path-page template:

1. **Starting-position profile** → template section 1 ("You are likely here if you believe")
2. **Core obstacle** → template section 2 ("The question your position cannot answer")
3. **Reading order** → template section 3 ("Read in this order")
4. **Primary objection + corpus answer** → template section 4 ("The specific objection the corpus answers")
5. **Off-ramp** → template section 5 ("When you've read these")
6. **Gate sequence** → used by the writer to understand *why* the reading order is what it is; not rendered directly

The **Gates** section (Part II) is shared reference material. Each path only crosses a subset of gates — the entry node tells you which, in what order.

## Part I — The Destination and the Non-Destination

### The Faithful-Abolitionist Terminus

For every Christian entry path, the destination is not merely "you changed your mind about abortion." Per **norman-statement** (Articles I–XI) and **fruits-of-abolitionism-is-true-repentance-necessary**, the terminus is a reader who:

- Has **repented** of prior apathy or compromise — not merely "course corrected" language (fruits-of-abolitionism)
- Affirms abortion is the unjust killing of a human being, grounded in the imago Dei (norman Art. I; faq §2)
- Holds **Scripture**, not polling/court/pragmatism, as the governing authority (norman Art. II, IV; biblical-not-secular; theology)
- Rejects gradualism: "less iniquity is still iniquity" (immediatism; norman Art. V)
- Rejects all exceptions: rape, incest, disability, duress (no-exceptions; a-mother-is-a-magistrate; faq §2 "What about rape and incest?")
- Accepts that the Church, not a parachurch or party, drives the work (norman Art. III; all-about-the-church; theology "The Church Possesses the Cure")
- Accepts full criminalization including equal protection that can reach culpable mothers (criminalization; a-mother-is-a-magistrate; faq §1 "Can't we end abortion without prosecuting mothers?"; norman Art. VII)
- Moves from belief to action: sidewalk ministry, magistrate engagement, church mobilization, legislative advocacy (biblical-not-secular; fruits-of-abolitionism)

### The Non-Christian Terminus (Distinct)

Per **faq §1 "Can People Who Disagree With You Join The Abolitionist Movement?"** and **norman-statement** Articles I, III, IV, XI:

> "Since the Abolitionist Movement is an explicitly Christian movement, it is impossible for us to partner with non-Christians, or for non-Christians to join the movement... three of the five tenets of abolitionism are: 1) Gospel-Centered 2) Biblical, and 3) Body driven/Obligation of the Church."

The anti-abortion non-Christian cannot be an abolitionist in this movement's sense. Their path terminates at **"repent and believe the gospel of Jesus Christ."** The corpus is unambiguous:

> "Dear reader, if this description fits you... you must repent of your sin, turn to Christ, and put your faith and trust in Jesus Christ alone for your salvation." (faq §1)

This must not be softened. The Task 10 writer ends the page at the gospel, not at activism steps or a sign-up form.

## Part II — The Six Decision Gates

These are the commitments each Christian entry path must cross. Paths do **not** all cross them in the same order, but every path must cross all six before arriving at the terminus. (The non-Christian path only crosses Gate 1 and a partial version of Gate 2 before being redirected to the gospel.)

### Gate 1 — Anthropology: Is abortion the unjust killing of a human being?

**Supporting articles:**

- **faq §2 "That's just your belief that life begins at fertilization/conception"** — dense block of embryology citations (Moore, Langman, Larsen, O'Rahilly, Carlson, Kischer). Use this to settle the scientific question without appealing to Scripture. Best single entry for a secular reader.
- **faq §2 "Isn't consciousness what makes us valuable?"** — answers the personhood-by-sentience move. Ties value to imago Dei; invokes the holocaust / chattel-slavery parallel for consciousness-based hierarchies.
- **abolitionism101** — establishes that the abolitionist position goes beyond "abortion is bad" to "abortion is murder." Short, foundational.
- **faq §2 "Isn't forced birth just like forced organ donation?"** — answers the bodily-autonomy rebuttal (four-point reply: ordinary vs. extraordinary care; parental creation of dependence; uterus as the organ designed for the child; the action-vs-refusal distinction).

### Gate 2 — Authority: By what standard do we make this claim?

**Supporting articles:**

- **biblical-not-secular** — the core thesis: Scripture vs. worldly wisdom. Includes Moses-as-first-abolitionist ("not a hoof shall be left behind"), Acts 5:29, and the Isaiah 10 framing.
- **theology** — deeper layer: abortion is *sin*, the cure is the *gospel*, and only those entrusted with the gospel (the Church) can truly push back the darkness. Explains why secular argumentation fails and why the movement is sola scriptura.
- **kristan-hawkins-flawed-reasoning-vs-scripture** — the "By what standard?" question made explicit. Exposes pragmatism ("save as many lives as possible") as a standard that, taken consistently, would justify clinic bombings. The reductio lands hard.
- **faq §1 "Since not everyone is a Christian, shouldn't we argue from a secular perspective?"** — Garrison quote: "Take away the Bible, and our warfare with oppression... is at an end."

### Gate 3 — Immediatism: Is "less iniquity" acceptable when true abolition is available?

**Supporting articles:**

- **immediatism** — the textual hinge of the whole corpus. Isaiah 10:1 ("Woe to those who decree iniquitous decrees"), Romans 3:8 (do not do evil that good may come), and the long Elizabeth Heyrick quote about gradual abolition as "the grand marplot of human virtue and happiness." Includes the Senator Brenner heartbeat-bill anecdote and the Norman mother quoting the Pain-Capable argument back at T. Russell Hunter.
- **against-pro-life-compromise-responding-to-denny-burk-andrew-walker-et-al** — the "abolitionism is not overnight-ism" correction. Uses the Garrison 1831 quote: "Urge immediate abolition as earnestly as we may, it will alas! be gradual abolition in the end." Critical for readers who've been told abolitionists are naive.
- **norman-statement Article V** — "incrementalism in theory is perpetuity in practice."
- **kristan-hawkins-flawed-reasoning-vs-scripture** — adds the Proverbs 20:23 / 11:1 "unequal weights and measures" hammer to the immediatism argument. Use in paths whose reader was persuaded by "save as many as possible."

### Gate 4 — No Exceptions: Are exceptions acceptable (rape, incest, disability, mother's life, duress)?

**Supporting articles:**

- **no-exceptions** — the doctrinal headline page. Heyrick's "spirit of accommodation and conciliation has been a spirit of delusion" quote. Covers rape, disability, and mother's-life-in-danger. Note: the source page has a structural quirk where the "Child Conceived in Rape" section contains boilerplate; the actual rape argument is the Heyrick quote plus the principle "we never write laws which make the fatherless prey" (see biblical-not-secular for the clearest rape-exception answer).
- **biblical-not-secular** — contains the sharpest single-line rape-exception answer: "we never write laws which make the fatherless prey and there's no one more fatherless than the child conceived in rape."
- **faq §2 "What about rape and incest?"** — "Rape is a terrible crime. It should be a capital crime... But it is insane and evil to murder a child for the sins of the father." Plus the incest/eugenics answer.
- **a-mother-is-a-magistrate-why-duress-is-no-defense-for-abortion** — the hardest-case exception. Distinguishes *physical* coercion (not her crime) from *volitional* coercion / duress (still her choice, still homicide). Blackstone: a magistrate "ought rather to die himself than escape by the murder of an innocent."

### Gate 5 — Obligation: Is belief enough, or must the Church act?

**Supporting articles:**

- **all-about-the-church** — "When the church is silent in the midst of a holocaust, she ceases to be a real church" (Sproul). Francis Schaeffer: "Every abortion clinic should have a sign in front of it saying, 'Open by the permission of the Church.'"
- **norman-statement Article III** — the Church, not a parachurch or party, is the primary driver. Pairs with Article VI on national repentance.
- **fruits-of-abolitionism-is-true-repentance-necessary** — attacks the "I've always been an abolitionist, I just didn't know it" off-ramp. Demands real repentance from prior apathy, not mere rebranding. The piece that closes the door on passive assent.
- **theology "The Church Possesses the Cure"** — Good Samaritan application; Luke 11:42 rebuke of the Pharisees ("neglect justice and the love of God").

### Gate 6 — How Action Manifests: Moral suasion, magistrate engagement, voting, criminalization

**Supporting articles:**

- **biblical-not-secular** (final section "Where They Conflict, Obey God Rather Than Man") — the concrete action list: sidewalk ministry at local killing centers, meetings with state magistrates, material aid for pregnant mothers, sharing abolitionist content, exhorting other Christians.
- **criminalization** — the legislative destination. Equal protection under the 14th Amendment, including the explicit refusal to grant blanket statutory immunity to mothers. Includes the Louisiana 2022 bill story and Cari Sietstra / NYT citation on the pro-life strategy creating a legal DIY abortion industry.
- **a-mother-is-a-magistrate-why-duress-is-no-defense-for-abortion** — holds the line on the hardest-to-swallow criminalization case.
- **faq §1 "Can't we end abortion without prosecuting mothers?"** + §3 "Under an abolition law, would every aborting mother be charged with 1st degree murder?" — the answer is nuanced: case-by-case based on facts (some 1st degree, some 3rd degree, some manslaughter, some declined to prosecute, some coerced and exempt). Not a blanket prosecution regime — but not blanket immunity either.

### Entry Node 1 — Secular Pro-Choice

**Starting-position profile (template section 1):**
- Abortion should be legal as a matter of personal autonomy ("my body, my choice")
- Personhood is not settled at conception; consciousness, viability, or sentience is what matters
- Religious arguments against abortion should not govern public policy in a pluralist society
- Banning abortion will just drive it underground and kill women in back-alley procedures
- "You can't legislate morality"

**Core obstacle (template section 2):**
The secular pro-choice position cannot survive contact with human embryology. Once the scientific question is conceded, the argument retreats to personhood-by-consciousness — which, applied consistently, yields eugenic conclusions about the cognitively disabled, infants, and the sleeping. Every fallback lands on a worse position than the one it fled.

**Gate sequence:**
1. Gate 1 (Anthropology) — settle the humanity of the preborn with embryology, not theology
2. Gate 1 again (personhood/bodily autonomy fallbacks) — close the consciousness and bodily-autonomy exits
3. Gate 2 (Authority) — reveal that even secular arguments ultimately rest on a moral standard
4. Gate 6 (soft introduction) — answer the "back-alley / can't-legislate-morality" pragmatic objections

Note: A secular pro-choice reader who is persuaded at Gates 1 and 2 becomes an anti-abortion non-Christian — they should then be handed off to Entry Node 7's path, **not** directly to the faithful-abolitionist terminus.

**Reading order:**

1. **faq** (§2, "Questions from a Pro-Choice perspective") — the entire section; but prioritize "That's just your belief that life begins at fertilization" and "Isn't consciousness what makes us valuable?" and "Isn't forced birth just like forced organ donation?" *Why:* These three answer the three strongest pro-choice moves in order: embryology denial, personhood-by-consciousness, bodily autonomy.
2. **abolitionism101** — *Why:* Establishes the "abortion is murder" frame so the reader understands what they're now confronting, not just "abortion is regrettable."
3. **faq** (§2, "You can't legislate morality" + "If abortion is criminalized, won't it just happen unsafely?" + "What about rape and incest?") — *Why:* Answers the three pragmatic objections that arise once the humanity question is lost.
4. **biblical-not-secular** — *Why:* The reader needs to see that the movement is openly, confessionally Christian. Do not disguise this.
5. **faq §1** ("Can People Who Disagree With You Join The Abolitionist Movement?") — *Why:* Sets up the handoff. The secular reader who concedes the argument now faces a worldview question, not a policy question.

**Primary objection (template section 4 — blockquote):**

> "You can have your religious opinion that abortion is wrong, but you can't impose it on everyone. The law has to be neutral between worldviews."

**Corpus answer (from faq §2 "You can't legislate morality"):** Every law legislates someone's morality. Speed limits, child-support laws, and homicide statutes are all moral claims. The question is never whether morality will be legislated but whose — and in the case of abortion, whose moral standard can actually ground the claim that killing is wrong. The secular frame cannot ground that claim objectively (faq §1, Garrison quote). The question "whose morality?" therefore collapses into "which standard can hold?"

**Off-ramp (template section 5):**
If the embryology and consciousness arguments have landed but the reader is not a Christian, hand off to Entry Node 7 (Anti-Abortion Non-Christian) — do not send to next-steps. The corpus is explicit: a non-Christian anti-abortion reader has a gospel appointment, not a legislative one.

### Entry Node 2 — Christian Pro-Choice / Progressive Christian

**Starting-position profile:**
- Identifies as Christian, often mainline/progressive
- Believes the Bible is ambiguous on abortion, or that "life begins" is a scientific/philosophical question Scripture doesn't address
- Frames abortion as an issue of mercy toward women, justice for the poor, or bodily autonomy
- Suspicious of "religious right" political involvement; prefers the Church to focus on spiritual matters
- May hold that criminalizing abortion is unloving or punitive

**Core obstacle:**
This reader holds two commitments that cannot both stand: that Scripture is authoritative, and that Scripture is silent or neutral on whether abortion is murder. The corpus does not persuade this reader by arguing louder; it confronts them with the actual texts (Exodus 21:22-25; Psalm 139; Luke 1:39-44; Proverbs 24:11; Isaiah 10:1-2) and with the category of sin — which cannot be addressed by mercy-talk alone.

**Gate sequence:**
1. Gate 2 (Authority) — Scripture is not silent; worldly-wise interpretation is disobedience
2. Gate 1 (Anthropology) — imago Dei and the preborn; the embryology confirms what Scripture assumes
3. Gate 5 (Obligation) — the Church cannot be silent during a holocaust; "mercy" without justice is neither
4. Gate 3 (Immediatism) — "delayed justice is denied justice"; no "compassionate" gradualism
5. Gate 6 (Action) — what love of neighbor actually requires

**Reading order:**

1. **theology** — *Why:* The deepest engagement with the Christian reader's own vocabulary: sin, gospel, kingdom, Church. This is where the progressive Christian meets the actual theological architecture of the movement.
2. **biblical-not-secular** — *Why:* Moses and Pharaoh; "not a hoof shall be left behind." Also names the Church's specific duty to magistrates, undercutting the "spiritual vs. political" split.
3. **all-about-the-church** — *Why:* Sproul and Schaeffer. The "silent church is not a real church" argument is the center of gravity for this reader. Abortion apathy is not neutral.
4. **norman-statement** — *Why:* Articles III, IV, V, VI (Church; Biblical Engagement; Immediate; National Repentance). The doctrinal core.
5. **fruits-of-abolitionism-is-true-repentance-necessary** — *Why:* Closes the "I was always on the right side really" rebranding exit. Demands actual repentance for prior apathy, not narrative repair.

**Primary objection:**

> "Christians should lead with grace, not law. Shaming women and criminalizing a difficult, private medical decision isn't what Jesus would do — he went to the margins. We shouldn't weaponize the state against frightened mothers."

**Corpus answer (from theology + all-about-the-church + fruits-of-abolitionism):** The gospel entails both the forgiveness of sin and the establishment of justice; these are not in tension (abolitionist-not-pro-life). A Church that refuses to name abortion as sin and to call for justice is not being merciful — it is being disobedient (Luke 11:42, "neglect justice and the love of God"). Schaeffer: every abortion clinic operates by the Church's permission. The "grace, not law" framing is a false dichotomy the corpus rejects at its root (Article V of the Norman Statement; Isaiah 1).

**Off-ramp:**
To **next-steps** (faithful-abolitionist terminus). This reader, if persuaded, is a Christian whose obedience has been misdirected, not someone outside the camp.

### Entry Node 3 — "Personally Opposed" Moderate

**Starting-position profile:**
- Personally believes abortion is wrong, at least in most cases
- Believes in "safe, legal, and rare" or some version thereof
- Would not have an abortion, would counsel a loved one against one — but opposes criminalization
- Frames the issue as a matter of personal conviction vs. public policy
- Often says "I'm not going to force my views on anyone else"

**Core obstacle:**
The "personally opposed" reader already concedes the humanity of the preborn (otherwise the "personal opposition" is unmotivated). Once humanity is conceded, the position collapses: no one says they are "personally opposed" to murdering toddlers but would not legislate against it. The inconsistency is fatal and the corpus is ruthless about exposing it.

**Gate sequence:**
1. Gate 1 (Anthropology) — surface the humanity the reader already tacitly grants
2. Gate 2 (Authority) — name the inconsistency: by what standard is this "personal"?
3. Gate 6 (Action) — law as instruction, restraint, and justice; legislation and personal conviction are not severable
4. Gate 3 (Immediatism) — "safe, legal, and rare" is structurally gradualist
5. Gate 5 (Obligation) — the private-conviction frame is itself a form of apathy

**Reading order:**

1. **abolitionism101** — *Why:* Short, foundational. Names the distinction between "abortion is bad" (the reader's current position) and "abortion is murder" (the position that compels legislation).
2. **abolitionist-not-pro-life** — *Why:* The Senator Brenner and Norman mother anecdotes show exactly where "personally opposed" reasoning lands in real legislation and in real mothers' words.
3. **kristan-hawkins-flawed-reasoning-vs-scripture** — *Why:* The three functions of civil law — instruction, restraint, justice. This is the section that dissolves the "personal vs. public" wall for the reader.
4. **faq §2 "You can't legislate morality"** + **§1 "Can't we end abortion without prosecuting mothers?"** — *Why:* Answers the two most common exit points from this position.
5. **fruits-of-abolitionism-is-true-repentance-necessary** — *Why:* The personally-opposed moderate is, in the corpus's framing, apathetic. This piece names that directly.

**Primary objection:**

> "I don't need the law to reflect my personal convictions. I'm against abortion for myself, but I don't want to force my beliefs on someone in a situation I haven't been in."

**Corpus answer (from kristan-hawkins-flawed-reasoning-vs-scripture + faq §2):** Civil law is inescapably didactic — it teaches. A law that permits abortion teaches that preborn children may be killed; a law that doesn't doesn't. "Personal opposition" to a murder you will not legislate against is not a coherent moral position; it is a private aesthetic preference dressed as ethics. You cannot be "personally opposed" to homicide while supporting its legality. If abortion is killing a human being, law must reflect it; if it is not, "personal opposition" is unmotivated. Pick one.

**Off-ramp:**
To **next-steps**.

### Entry Node 4 — Pro-Life with Exceptions (rape, incest, mother's life)

**Starting-position profile:**
- Believes abortion is the killing of a human being and should generally be illegal
- Holds exceptions for rape, incest, and/or cases where the mother's life is threatened
- Supports laws like 15-week or heartbeat bills "as progress" while allowing these exceptions
- Often framed as compassion for the woman in extreme circumstances
- May be persuaded by "saving more lives" pragmatism

**Core obstacle:**
Exception-holding exposes the reader's position as inconsistent with its own premise. If the preborn is a human being, then the crime of the father (rape, incest) does not license killing the child; the circumstances of conception do not alter the child's nature; and "mother's life" cases can be addressed by early delivery without intentional killing. The exception itself is what undermines the anti-abortion argument in public, legal, and theological terms — Roe's Footnote 54 exploited this exact inconsistency.

**Gate sequence:**
1. Gate 1 (Anthropology) — re-confirm: the child's humanity does not depend on the father's crime or the mother's distress
2. Gate 4 (No Exceptions) — the central gate for this reader
3. Gate 2 (Authority) — exceptions reveal a pragmatic, not biblical, standard
4. Gate 3 (Immediatism) — the "exceptions for some wins" logic is incrementalism in miniature
5. Gate 6 (Action) — what full criminalization, mercy included, actually looks like

**Reading order (this is the template path — use as the model for the other six):**

1. **no-exceptions** — *Why:* Direct, head-on. Heyrick on accommodation as delusion; the principle that every yielded iota impairs conviction of rectitude.
2. **biblical-not-secular** — *Why:* Delivers the sharpest one-line rape-exception answer: "we never write laws which make the fatherless prey and there's no one more fatherless than the child conceived in rape."
3. **faq §2 "What about rape and incest?"** + **"What about children who will be born into poverty or suffering?"** — *Why:* The short, hammer-blow versions of the exception answers; the incest/eugenics clarifier.
4. **a-mother-is-a-magistrate-why-duress-is-no-defense-for-abortion** — *Why:* Applies the no-exceptions logic to the hardest case (duress) and introduces the criminalization principle from the child's side of the equation.
5. **immediatism** — *Why:* Once exceptions fall, the reader needs to see the structural parallel between "exceptions" and "incremental bills." Same argument, same failure mode, same Heyrick diagnosis.

**Primary objection:**

> "I can't look a rape victim in the face and tell her she has to carry her rapist's child. That's cruel. There have to be some exceptions — otherwise we're just ideologues."

**Corpus answer (from no-exceptions + biblical-not-secular + faq §2 "What about rape and incest?"):** The child conceived in rape is the most fatherless among us — Scripture reserves special protection for the fatherless (Deut 24:17; Exodus 22:22-24). To write the child's death into law because of his father's crime is to make the fatherless prey. "Rape is a terrible crime. It should be a capital crime... But it is insane and evil to murder a child for the sins of the father." The appeal to not-being-cruel collapses once the child's humanity is admitted: we do not kill a one-year-old born of rape to comfort the mother. The exception is cruelty to a different person, hidden from view.

**Off-ramp:**
To **next-steps**.

### Entry Node 5 — Pro-Life Incrementalist (agrees in principle, defends "small wins")

**Starting-position profile:**
- Agrees abortion is the killing of a human being and should be abolished
- Believes "we can't get there in one step" and that heartbeat bills, 15-week bans, pain-capable bills are real victories
- Frames opposition to incrementalism as letting the perfect be the enemy of the good
- Cites "save as many lives as possible" as the operating principle
- May believe abolitionists are naive, utopian, or "overnight-ists"

**Core obstacle:**
This reader has accepted the pragmatic syllogism: save as many as possible → incremental laws save some → therefore incremental laws are justified. That syllogism cannot survive two tests. First, the *reductio* (kristan-hawkins): if the standard is only outcomes, clinic bombings would be justified if they deterred abortions. Pro-lifers rightly reject that — which means they already recognize a higher standard than mere outcomes. Second, incremental bills codify the acceptability of killing children outside the bill's threshold (heartbeat, pain-capable, viability) — they are not failed abolition bills, they are successful iniquitous decrees.

**Gate sequence:**
1. Gate 2 (Authority) — "by what standard?" — pragmatism fails its own consistency test
2. Gate 3 (Immediatism) — the central gate; requires depth
3. Gate 4 (No Exceptions) — exceptions and incrementalism share a logic
4. Gate 6 (Action) — criminalization including mothers (often the actual sticking point behind pro-life incrementalism — see the Louisiana 2022 story)
5. Gate 5 (Obligation) — repent of apathy / compromise, not rebrand

**Reading order:**

1. **immediatism** — *Why:* The load-bearing text. Heyrick at length; the Senator Brenner and Norman mother stories make the "unintended-consequence" objection impossible to sustain.
2. **against-pro-life-compromise-responding-to-denny-burk-andrew-walker-et-al** — *Why:* Directly answers the "you're just overnight-ists" charge with Garrison's 1831 line ("Urge immediate abolition as earnestly as we may, it will alas! be gradual abolition in the end"). This is the piece that lets the reader keep their intellectual self-respect while changing their position.
3. **kristan-hawkins-flawed-reasoning-vs-scripture** — *Why:* The "by what standard?" hammer. The three functions of civil law. Exposes that the real operating principle inside SFLA is "mothers are second victims," not "save as many as possible."
4. **criminalization** — *Why:* The Louisiana 2022 story. Why the pro-life establishment in actual practice has opposed abolition bills — they are not the allies the reader thinks they are.
5. **fruits-of-abolitionism-is-true-repentance-necessary** — *Why:* Once the incrementalist sees the issue, they need to not do the "I've always been an abolitionist, I just didn't know it" move. The piece names and refuses that exit.

**Primary objection:**

> "Saving some babies is better than saving none. You abolitionists oppose heartbeat bills and then brag about purity while babies die. That's not a moral high ground — that's vanity."

**Corpus answer (from immediatism + against-pro-life-compromise + kristan-hawkins):** Incremental bills do not save the babies the reader imagines. They codify the legal permissibility of killing younger children, train the culture that heartbeat/pain-capable thresholds are what matter (see the young mother in Norman: "My baby won't feel any pain"), and divert the Church's finite political energy away from actual abolition. Louisiana 2022: when an abolition bill came to the floor, pro-life organizations killed it. The choice is not "some lives vs. no lives" — it is "iniquitous decrees that preserve legal abortion vs. bills that end it." Garrison: "Urge immediate abolition as earnestly as we may, it will alas! be gradual abolition in the end. We have never said that slavery would be overthrown by a single blow; that it ought to be we shall always contend." Abolitionism is not overnight-ism — it is the demand without which the gradual victory never comes.

**Off-ramp:**
To **next-steps**.

### Entry Node 6 — Apathetic Christian (thinks abortion is wrong, doesn't engage)

**Starting-position profile:**
- Confesses Christ; attends church; affirms abortion is wrong
- Does not talk about abortion publicly, does not engage legislatively, does not go to abortion mills
- Believes politics is divisive; the gospel is spiritual; the Church should focus on discipleship
- May donate to a pro-life organization and consider the duty discharged
- Assumes the issue is "being handled" by others

**Core obstacle:**
This reader's silence is not spiritually neutral. The corpus treats apathy as sin — specifically the sin of the priest and Levite in the Good Samaritan parable, and the sin rebuked in Luke 11:42 ("you tithe mint and rue and neglect justice"). A Christian who will not bring the gospel into conflict with the culture of death is, in the corpus's framing, disobedient, not merely under-informed.

**Gate sequence:**
1. Gate 5 (Obligation) — the central gate; must go first for this reader
2. Gate 2 (Authority) — name Scripture's explicit commands regarding magistrates and the preborn
3. Gate 3 (Immediatism) — counter the "I support the pro-life movement with a monthly donation" off-ramp
4. Gate 6 (Action) — what love of preborn neighbor concretely looks like
5. Gate 5 again (repentance) — close the rebrand exit with fruits-of-abolitionism

**Reading order:**

1. **all-about-the-church** — *Why:* Sproul: silent church / not a real church. Schaeffer: "Open by the permission of the Church." This is the sermon that addresses exactly the reader's posture.
2. **theology** — *Why:* "The Church Possesses the Cure." Good Samaritan applied to the local killing center, OBGYN office, mail-order abortion pills; Luke 11:42 applied to the Christian who tithes and neglects.
3. **biblical-not-secular** — *Why:* The "Obey God Rather Than Man" section lists the specific duties: sidewalk ministry, meetings with magistrates, aid to pregnant mothers, social-media witness, exhorting other Christians.
4. **fruits-of-abolitionism-is-true-repentance-necessary** — *Why:* The piece that refuses the "I've always been an abolitionist, I just didn't know it" language-swap. Demands real repentance of prior apathy.
5. **norman-statement** (especially Articles III, VI, VIII) — *Why:* Places the reader inside a doctrinal statement they can sign and be held to. The movement isn't asking for sympathy; it's asking for a pledge.

**Primary objection:**

> "I believe abortion is wrong. I'm not called to political activism — my calling is discipleship / family / my vocation. There are lots of ways to serve the Lord, and this isn't mine."

**Corpus answer (from all-about-the-church + theology + fruits-of-abolitionism):** The Good Samaritan parable establishes that the duty to rescue those being led to slaughter is not a special calling but a universal one — the priest and Levite had other duties too, and their passing by was sin. Abortion is not one cause among many; it is a present holocaust. R.C. Sproul: a silent church ceases to be a real church. Every Christian has a sphere of influence; silence within it is not prudence, it is complicity. The "not my calling" framing is, in the corpus's language, the apathy Jesus rebuked in Luke 11:42.

**Off-ramp:**
To **next-steps**. This reader's terminus is identical to the others' but their journey is shaped by the fact that their resistance is volitional, not intellectual.

### Entry Node 7 — Anti-Abortion Non-Christian

**Starting-position profile:**
- Agrees abortion is wrong, possibly that it is murder
- Not a Christian: may be Catholic (in the corpus's sense — see faq §1), Muslim, Mormon, Jehovah's Witness, atheist, agnostic, or hold a worldview the corpus classifies as opposed to the biblical gospel
- May have supported abolition bills, attended rallies, donated to anti-abortion organizations
- Wants to be recognized as part of the movement

**Core obstacle:**
This is the one path where the core obstacle is **not** abortion doctrine at all. The movement's own FAQ and Norman Statement make it explicit that three of the five tenets (Gospel-Centered, Biblical, Church-Driven) are incompatible with non-Christian commitment. The reader may agree with abolitionism's conclusions without being able to hold its premises; partial agreement on conclusions does not constitute abolitionism. The terminus is not "join us" but **"repent and believe the gospel."**

**Gate sequence:**
1. Gate 1 (Anthropology) — acknowledged / affirmed (this reader already grants it via common grace)
2. Gate 2 (Authority) — the point of divergence: the reader cannot ground their opposition to abortion in anything objective without Christ
3. **Terminus: the gospel** — not Gates 3–6

The reader does not cross Gates 3–6. Those gates describe the life of someone inside the covenant community. The path ends at the foot of the cross.

**Reading order:**

1. **abolitionism101** — *Why:* Sets expectations. The reader sees immediately that this is a confessional Christian movement.
2. **theology** — *Why:* The piece most likely to create cognitive dissonance between "I oppose abortion" and "I do not believe the gospel." Specifically: abortion is sin, the only cure for sin is the gospel, and only those entrusted with the gospel can push back the darkness.
3. **norman-statement** (Articles I, III, IV, XI) — *Why:* The formal doctrinal lines that make the partnership question unambiguous. Article XI in particular is the gospel itself.
4. **faq §1 "Can People Who Disagree With You Join The Abolitionist Movement?"** — *Why:* This is the single most important text for this path. It names the reader's position directly, explains why the movement cannot recognize them as an abolitionist, and issues the gospel call in plain language: "you must repent of your sin, turn to Christ, and put your faith and trust in Jesus Christ alone for your salvation."

**Primary objection:**

> "Abortion is wrong — I agree with you on that. I support your bills, I go to your rallies. Why won't you call me an abolitionist? You're being sectarian when we should be united against a great evil."

**Corpus answer (from faq §1, directly):** "Holding some ideas that are part of the abolitionist position does not make you an abolitionist, any more than a Roman Catholic agreeing with a protestant that Jesus Christ is God and the Son of God does not make the Roman Catholic a Protestant." The movement's measure of success is not lives saved, bills passed, or hearts changed; it is faithful obedience to Jesus Christ. A worldview that opposes Christ cannot, even in its best moments, stand with a movement whose entire foundation is Christ's kingdom. The reader's anti-abortion conviction is common grace — the law of God written on the heart — but to embrace that conviction while rejecting the Christ who authored it is, per the corpus, hypocrisy.

**Off-ramp:**
**The gospel, not next-steps.** The Task 10 page ends with Article XI of the Norman Statement (or equivalent plain-speech gospel presentation) and a call to repent and believe. It does **not** end with a sign-up form, a petition, or an activism ladder. A reader who responds in faith becomes a Christian first, then can re-enter the journey through Entry Node 2 or 6 as appropriate.

## Part V — Mental Walkthrough (self-check)

I walked each path as specified above and flag the following for Task 4–10 writers:

### Strong paths (confidence: high)
- **Node 4 (Pro-Life with Exceptions)** — the corpus has the tightest, most redundant answers here. `no-exceptions`, `biblical-not-secular`, `faq §2`, `a-mother-is-a-magistrate`, and `immediatism` cover every exit the reader can take. This is the cleanest template.
- **Node 5 (Pro-Life Incrementalist)** — `immediatism` + `kristan-hawkins` + `against-pro-life-compromise` + `criminalization` form a nearly airtight argument. The Louisiana 2022 story is the single most persuasive piece of evidence in the corpus for this reader.
- **Node 7 (Anti-Abortion Non-Christian)** — `faq §1` is explicitly written for this reader. There is no ambiguity in the terminus.

### Medium-strength paths (confidence: adequate, but watch for these gaps)
- **Node 2 (Christian Pro-Choice / Progressive Christian)** — the corpus does not contain a single article that engages progressive Christianity on its own terms (e.g., the hermeneutical assumptions behind "Jesus would be on the side of women"). Task 7 writer should lean hard on `theology` and `all-about-the-church`, and let the Scripture-density of those pieces do the work. If the writer feels the path is thin, the answer is not to extend the reading list — it is to trust the texts and be blunt. This reader is not persuaded by volume; they are confronted by exegesis.
- **Node 6 (Apathetic Christian)** — `all-about-the-church` and the "Church Possesses the Cure" section of `theology` are the emotional and theological core. The risk here is that the reader nods through the articles without acting, which is exactly the failure mode `fruits-of-abolitionism` anticipates. Task 9 writer should make the off-ramp to next-steps feel inevitable, not optional.

### Weaker paths (confidence: workable but flag to the writer)
- **Node 1 (Secular Pro-Choice)** — the corpus is a confessional Christian document; it does not pretend to be secular. The reading list depends heavily on `faq §2`, which is strong on embryology but does not thoroughly engage secular political philosophy (Rawls, Dworkin, etc.). Task 6 writer: do not try to make the corpus speak in a voice it doesn't have. The path's honesty is part of its witness. A secular reader who concedes the argument is being handed a worldview, not a policy preference — and the piece that names this openly is `faq §1`, which is why it closes the list.
- **Node 3 (Personally Opposed)** — the corpus doesn't have a dedicated article for this reader; the path is stitched from `abolitionism101`, `abolitionist-not-pro-life`, `kristan-hawkins`, and selected FAQ entries. Task 8 writer: the strongest move is to name the inconsistency ruthlessly in template section 2 ("The question your position cannot answer") and let the corpus pieces land the blows. Do not soften the central charge: "personally opposed" to homicide while supporting its legality is not a position, it is a posture.

### Global note for all writers
The corpus does not apologize for its doctrinal maximalism, and neither should the path pages. Softening language (e.g., "some abolitionists believe," "this is one perspective") will produce pages that do not match their source material and will confuse readers who then click through to the articles. The corpus is confessional; the path pages must carry that confession forward intact.
